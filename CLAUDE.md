# my-content-system

A content design system based on Tesco's Blueberry design system principles. All content produced in this project must follow these rules.

> Source: [Tesco Blueberry Content Design System](https://www.tesco.design/6408f7e25/p/63786f-welcome-to-blueberry)

## Project Structure

- `skills/` — Custom skills for content workflows
  - `content-checker.md` — 10-step content review against all Blueberry rules (auto-activates on content edits)
  - `copy-creator.md` — 10-step guided content creation with full brief output
  - `tone-checker.md` — Quick-reference checklist (legacy, superseded by content-checker)
  - `learn.md` — Captures learnings and patterns from content reviews
- `memory.md` — Persistent context and decisions

## 1. The 5 Content Design Principles

All content must be shaped by these 5 principles:

### HUMAN
Language written how we speak to each other. To our friends. Our parents. And our children. Words for everyone, giving meaning to an experience designed for all.

### CLEAR
Simple words that are easy to understand. That explain, guide and help. That make experiences easy, instant and accessible.

### INCLUSIVE
Content that cares who you are, where you live and what you need to do to live a better life. That brings us together, not regardless of our differences but because of them.

### HELPFUL
User-focused content with a purpose. That gives you a reason to be here. And a choice of simple ways to get your job done.

### ENGAGING
Experiences that add value to your day. Moments that delight, inform and strengthen a brand that people know and love. On whichever platform they find them.

## 2. Voice and Tone

### Core Traits
- **Helpfulness** — Lead with this. Speak in a simple, conversational way to make things as easy as possible.
- **Brand personality** — When appropriate, dial up positive, playful and witty energy.

### Key Distinction
- **Voice** is consistent — how we sound day in and day out. It stays the same.
- **Tone** changes depending on context — exciting news vs. apologising, browsing vs. paying.

### Tone Calibration by Context
| Context | Lean towards |
|---------|-------------|
| Browsing, discovery | More personality/playfulness |
| Searching for something specific | More helpfulness |
| Booking/scheduling | Helpfulness — reduce cognitive load |
| Payment | Balance — milestone moment but high-stakes |
| Deals/savings | More personality — exciting moment |
| Returns/errors | Helpfulness — empathy, resolve quickly |

### Why Voice and Tone Matter
1. **Connection** — Human, relatable language creates loyalty
2. **Trust** — Unified voice across the journey creates authenticity
3. **Distinction** — Our voice makes us stand out in a crowded market

## 3. How We Create Good Content

### Write with Purpose
- Meet two sets of goals: the goals of the business AND the goals of the people using the experience
- Understand the strategic context before writing

### Design with Words
Good UX writing serves four purposes:
- **Context** — Where am I? How did I get here?
- **Feedback** — What just happened? Why?
- **Guidance** — What do I need to know? What helps me progress?
- **Action** — What can I do here?

If your content doesn't answer one or more of these, you may not be fulfilling a real user need.

### Write Like You Speak
- Plain, simple, everyday language
- A short word is almost always better than a long one
- Read it back — would you actually say that? Would an 8-year-old understand?
- If it feels awkward to say or read, simplify it

### Use More Verbs
- People come to do something — "doing words" are essential
- CTAs, text links, and headings should start with a verb
- Body copy should be full of verbs and active language
- Examples: Shop, Book, Collect, Save, Spend, Add, Pay, Get, Discover, Start, Tap, Request

### Create Consistency
- Use the same language across all touchpoints
- Don't use multiple words for the same action (e.g., pick ONE of: "View more" / "See more" / "Show more")
- Before writing, check if similar content already exists
- "Product" = a unique thing you can buy; "Item" = the number of products you have

### Build Choices on Data
- Validate word choices with A/B tests
- Check customer language on feedback channels and social media
- Check readability scores
- Use the Research Repository as your first stop
- Iterate — improving copy is natural

### Start a Conversation
- UX copy is a conversation between the experience and the user
- CTAs are the user speaking — they should complete the sentence "I want to..."
  - "I want to... pay now"
  - "I want to... add to basket"
  - "I want to... request vouchers"

### Don't Make People Think
- People don't read — they scan for information relevant to their personal mission
- They are busy, hungry, tired, stressed, distracted
- Use language as a **consistent pattern** to increase comprehension
- Never break these patterns — inconsistency forces people to think about words instead of choices

**Bad example:**
1. using my card
2. I want to pay with vouchers
3. You want to choose a different method

**Good example:**
- Use my registered card
- Use vouchers
- Add a different method of payment

### Make Content Scannable
- Use headings, subheadings, and **bold** to draw the eye
- Use bullet points for lists and multiple points
- Keep sentences short
- Separate paragraphs for easier scanning
- Headings and CTAs are the two things people read most — make them work hard

### Reuse Copy Components
- Check the content library before writing new copy
- Benefits: saves time, creates consistency, copy is already approved
- Caveats: write new copy if a component doesn't quite work; consistency for its own sake isn't the aim

### Think About Localisation
- Content may need to work in multiple languages
- A 5-character English word might be 10 characters in another language
- Collaborate with international writers and designers

### Run It By a Content Designer
- Before sending to build, get a content designer to review
- They tighten copy, sharpen writing, and ensure every word works hard

## 4. Accessible Writing Best Practices

### Links and Buttons
- Write descriptive links (e.g., "Contact us", not "Click here" or "Read more")
- Link text must make sense **out of context** — screen reader users skip between links
- All links on a page should be unique
- Front-load link text with the most important words first
- Keep CTAs to 2-4 words ideally
- Start CTAs with a verb (go, start, edit, add, book, view)
- Link/button text should match the destination page name
- Use sentence case — easier to scan, less cognitive load
- Icon-only buttons need a text alternative
- Avoid mid-sentence links — they slow scan-reading

### Arranging Content
- Break up content with sub-headings, images, videos
- Use bullet points that:
  - Complete a sentence
  - Are front-loaded with the most important info
  - Start with the same language element (verb, noun, adjective)
  - Use correct grammar
- Keep paragraphs short — one idea per sentence
- Align text to the left with consistent layout (helps dyslexia)
- Don't put too much information in one place

### Headings and Labels
- Main page headings are H1s
- Always use correct heading hierarchy (H2 followed by H3, never skip levels)
- Every page/screen should have a unique heading
- Write descriptive headings — they should make sense in isolation
- Keep headings under 65 characters (Google truncates after ~65)
- Don't use questions in headings — "Frontloading headings" beats "Should I frontload my headings?"
- All form fields must have a label
- Avoid title case — sentence case is 13-18% easier to read
- Never use block capitals — it reads as shouting

### Use Plain Language
- Aim for Hemingway grade 6 or lower (reading age of an 11-year-old)
- 1 in 6 UK adults have very low literacy skills
- 10% of the UK population has some level of dyslexia
- Use active voice, not passive
- Use present tense
- Use short, simple words (buy not purchase, help not assist, around not approximately)
- Avoid figures of speech and idioms
- Don't build walls of text
- Break up long sentences — 15 words ideal, 25 words maximum
- Use common, everyday words
- Be consistent with terminology
- Explain technical terms the first time you use them

### Errors
- Write in simple language, not error codes
- Use active language ("Enter the correct date format" not "The wrong date format has been entered")
- Focus on fixing the problem, not the problem itself
- Place errors below the form field they apply to
- Use a friendly tone but don't be jokey or say "Ooops"
- Never blame the user

### Text Alternatives
- All written text needs a basic alternative (alt text, transcript, audio description)
- Use actual text, never images of text
- Alt text should be clear, accurate, and concise
- No alt text needed for purely decorative images
- Icons and icon-only buttons need alt text

### Media
- Add closed captions and transcripts to all videos
- Provide transcripts for podcasts and audio
- Check automated captions for accuracy
- Add audio descriptions for videos with important visuals

### Forms
- Use descriptive labels that describe the function
- Avoid placeholder text — use hint text instead
- Break long forms into shorter, multi-page ones
- Only label optional fields — don't mark everything as (required)

### General Dos and Don'ts
- Don't force users to remember things from previous pages
- Mix short and long sentences for natural flow
- Don't use ampersands (&) except in logos — "and" is easier to understand
- Avoid FAQs — put important information in-journey instead
- Don't follow strict grammar conventions if breaking them is clearer
- Be short and concise ("Total cost" not "This is the total cost")
- You typically don't need the word "now" (say "apply" not "apply now")
- Avoid Latin (eg, ie, etc, per annum) — use "for example", "such as", "that is", "each year", "a month"

## 5. Inclusive Language

### Core Principle
The words we choose shape how people feel. Inclusive language helps everyone feel welcome, represented and respected.

### Tips
- Use diverse names in examples (e.g., Blair, Harsh, Quynh)
- Avoid metaphors and idioms that could confuse non-native speakers or neurodivergent users
- Use "blocklist" and "allowlist" instead of "blacklist" and "whitelist"
- Use "primary", "main", or "source" instead of "master"
- Don't call characters like à or š "special" — they're standard in many languages
- Don't rely on shape, size, colour, or position alone for meaning

### Writing About Identity

**Gender:**
- Use gender-neutral pronouns (they, you, we)
- Avoid "manpower", "mankind" — use "staffing", "humankind"
- Use "attended/unattended tills" not "manned/unmanned tills"
- Ask for "pronouns" not "preferred pronouns"
- Don't include "other" as a gender option — let people self-describe or skip
- Question whether you need titles (Mr/Mrs) at all

**Faith and Religion:**
- Be respectful and neutral. Avoid assumptions or stereotypes.

**Age:**
- Prioritise readability. Use clear, simple language.

**Race:**
- Don't use black, white, dark, or light as value-laden metaphors
- "Dark mode" and "light mode" are fine (no value judgement)

**Disability:**
- Avoid "disabled button" — use "inactive" or "not available"
- Don't use euphemisms like "differently abled"
- Avoid "fast", "quick", or "easy" — say "this usually takes 2 minutes"
- Use person-first language ("disabled people", not "the disabled")
- Avoid negative framing ("suffering from", "confined to a wheelchair")
- Think of mobility aids as tools, not limitations

## 6. Emoji Guidelines

- Use emojis, not emoticons (emojis have alt text for screen readers)
- Never use emojis to replace words
- Don't repeat emojis or use too many
- Place emojis **after** written content, not before or mid-sentence
- Use popular, widely understood emojis
- Check emojis work in both light and dark mode
- Be careful with facial expression emojis — not universally understood
- Yellow skin tone doesn't mean "neutral" — consider representation
- Emojis should reinforce meaning, not replace words

## 7. Messaging Patterns

### General Messaging Rules
- **Be clear** — simple language over clever phrasing
- **Be helpful** — include: what happened, why, what happens next
- **Start strong** — most important information first
- **Keep it short** — include relevant info and nothing else
- **Focus on one thing** — don't overload with multiple issues
- **Consider context** — place messages where users will see them
- **Focus on action** — provide a way to fix problems
- **Sound human** — use customer language, not jargon
- **Show empathy** — warm, caring language when things go wrong (apologise sparingly, only if our fault)
- **Be consistent** — same words and phrases as the rest of the experience
- **Include timescales** — if there's a deadline, state it

### Error Messages
- Keep things calm — avoid "failure", "broken", even "error"
- Be specific but relevant — explain in user language, not technical terms
- Never blame the user
- Structure: heading (what's wrong) + body (why + how to fix) + CTA (action to resolve)

### Warning Messages
- Go light on "please" and "sorry" — don't say please if the action isn't optional; don't apologise for things that aren't our fault
- Tell them what to do next
- Never use capitals or exclamation marks for emphasis — they add stress

### Success Messages
- Give positive feedback — confirm what the user did
- Don't use the word "successfully" — it rarely adds anything
- Add delight sometimes, but never at the expense of clarity

### Information Messages
- Use sparingly — only tell users things they truly need to know
- Link to more information when space is limited

### Amend Messages
- Include the deadline clearly
- Put the message in multiple places if it's important
- Don't disappoint — if you say they can do something, make sure they can
- Avoid anxiety — don't use "hurry" or "don't miss out"

## 8. Content Library — Component-Level Guidance

### Modals
- Modals interrupt the user — use sparingly and only when necessary
- **When to use:** user must make an active decision, or confirm an irreversible/sensitive action
- **When NOT to use:** non-essential info, anything needing lots of context, anything that could be in-page messaging
- **Writing rules:**
  - Get to the point quickly — heading + first sentence must state what's happening
  - Make actions clear with specific verbs — avoid "Yes" and "No"
  - Avoid "cancel" confusion in destructive actions — use "Keep [thing]", "Never mind", or "Not now" instead
  - Choose brevity over personality
  - Be extra concise in native (iOS/Android) modals
- **Templates:**
  - Confirmation: heading = [Action]? / body = This will [consequence] / buttons = [Action verb + object] | Cancel
  - Permission: heading = Allow [permission]? / body = We use [permission] to [reason] / buttons = Allow | Not now
  - Multiple choice: heading = Select [choice] / body = [explanation + form elements] / buttons = Continue | Cancel

### Buttons
- Buttons are for actions — they represent the user's intent
- **Writing rules:**
  - Start with a verb in present tense
  - Keep to 2-3 words, max 4
  - Avoid figurative language — direct, literal language only
  - Use sentence case (capitalise first word + proper nouns only)
  - Button copy must make sense on its own without surrounding context
- **Use links, not buttons**, when sending users to another page for information
- **Sign in / Sign out** — not "log in" / "log out"
- Loyalty-specific verbs are fixed: always "collect points" (not "earn" or "get")

### Links
- Links navigate — they take users somewhere. Buttons trigger actions.
- **Writing rules:**
  - Clearly describe the destination — be specific about where the link goes
  - Avoid "Click here", "Read more", "More info" — vague and inaccessible
  - Front-load meaning — most important words first
  - Keep under 5 words ideally, 6-8 max
  - Avoid mid-sentence links
  - Be consistent — same phrasing for the same destination across the experience
- **Verb patterns:**
  - "View..." — exploring information/details
  - "Go to..." — switching to a dashboard or functional area
  - "Browse..." — exploring ranges or collections
  - "Find..." — discovery tools or locators
  - "Learn more about..." — educational/explanatory content
  - "Read..." — long-form content, reports, policies

### Ratings
- Ratings let users give feedback — write neutrally, never lead
- **Writing rules:**
  - Keep it brief — rating is rarely the user's primary goal
  - Label clearly — title should specify what's being rated
  - Use question format ("How would you rate...?") or instruction ("Rate your experience of...")
  - Avoid emotionally-charged descriptions for each star — use "very satisfied/dissatisfied" or "poor/excellent"
  - For non-interactive ratings: always pair visual stars with written text ("5 stars") and show number of reviewers
- **Template:** "How would you rate {process/product/service}?" or "Rate your experience of {process/product/service}"

### Popovers
- Small contextual overlays that explain, clarify, or support — more subtle than modals
- **When to use:** additional context, explaining unfamiliar terms, highlighting new features
- **When NOT to use:** vital task-completion info (make that permanently visible), long/complex content (link to another page instead)
- **Writing rules:**
  - 1-3 lines max. No full stops in headings; in body, only use them if >8 words or multiple sentences
  - Only add useful info that isn't already on the page
  - If heading + CTA exist, the heading should match the CTA action
  - Default to body-copy-only popovers — add heading/button only if genuinely needed
- **Templates:**
  - Explanation: "{Term} means {brief description}" or "This {description of functionality}"
  - Guidance: "{Brief instruction} + {benefit}" — e.g., "Tap the heart to save products to your list"
  - Reassurance: "You won't be charged until..." or "We'll save your preferences..."

### Loading Spinners
- Reassure users something is happening — set expectations and keep things moving
- **Writing rules:**
  - Clearly state what's happening — avoid generic "loading" or "please wait"
  - Be specific but not technical — explain in user language
  - Use present tense — "Updating your basket" not "We're going to update your basket"
  - Keep it brief — a few words or one sentence
  - Set time expectations only if reliable — don't over-promise
  - Show progress for longer waits — "75% complete" or step-by-step updates
  - Flex tone thoughtfully — playfulness OK for rewards, not for payments
- **Templates:**
  - Basic: "Verb + object" — "Placing your order", "Verifying card details"
  - With time: "Verb + object + expectation" — "Checking stock. This may take a few minutes."
  - With warning: "Verb + object + instruction" — "Processing payment. Don't refresh this page."

## 9. Writing for Screen Readers

- Screen readers turn digital content into speech or Braille for blind/visually impaired users
- They announce role and state of elements ("Radio button, selected") and interaction hints ("Text field, double-tap to edit")

### Key Rules
- **Describe images** — write alt text for all visual elements carrying information (icons, logos, image buttons, graphs, maps, screenshots)
- **Provide transcripts** — for videos and audio content, structured with headings
- **Use correct heading hierarchy** — h1, h2, h3 in order; never skip levels; screen reader users navigate by jumping between headings
- **Make content scannable** — break up long content, most important information first
- **Avoid directional language** — don't say "above", "below", "left", "right"
- **Avoid sensory-only instructions** — don't say "tap the green button"; describe the function instead ("Select Check Out")
- **Use "select" not "click"** — inclusive of all navigation methods (clicking, tapping, pressing, keyboard)
- **Watch for heteronyms** — words with same spelling but different pronunciation (read, live, close, content, invalid). Replace with unambiguous alternatives when possible
- **Punctuation** — stick to commas and full stops; avoid semi-colons, dashes, brackets, asterisks
- **Acronyms** — generally avoid abbreviations to prevent misinterpretation, except universally understood ones (PDF, FAQ)
- **Descriptive links** — link destination must be clear from just the link text; screen reader users navigate links out of context
- **Test with a screen reader** — JAWS/NVDA (PC), VoiceOver (Mac/iOS), TalkBack (Android)
- **Write for everyone** — correct punctuation, spelling, grammar; standard conventions; appropriate language

## 10. Conversation Design (AI/Chatbots)

### Grice's Maxims for Good Conversations
1. **Quality** — Provide high quality info and don't lie
2. **Quantity** — Give the right amount of info — not too little, not too much
3. **Relation** — Give relevant info. Stay on topic
4. **Manner** — Organise your thoughts, don't ramble, use clear language

### Conversation Design Principles
- **Active over passive voice** — "To help you replace your Clubcard, I'll ask you a few simple questions" not "you'll be asked a few simple questions"
- **Simple words** — don't use jargon; a child should be able to understand
- **Clarity over detail** — be clear and to the point; limited cognitive load especially on voice
- **If it sounds written, rewrite it** — read aloud; if it sounds written, it's not conversational
- **Pass the one breath test** — if you can't say the message in one breath, it's too long
- **Anticipate need for elaboration** — when the user is upset or may need more info, don't be afraid to elaborate
- **Consistent terminology** — don't confuse users by using different terms for the same thing
- **Never blame the user** — when something goes wrong, don't imply it's the user's fault; handle miscommunications gracefully

### AI Personality
- Users will construct a personality for the AI whether you design one or not — so design it intentionally
- Only include traits relevant to the interaction (accent, attitude) — not irrelevant personal details
- Key traits: friendly, empathetic, helpful
- For AI: use "virtual assistant" or "chat assistant"; for humans: use "agent"
- Target feedback: "She went above and beyond to help me", "It was like talking to a human", "The customer service did exactly what I needed"

## 11. Glossary — Terminology and Style Rules

Quick-reference for word choices, formatting, and house style. When in doubt, check here first.

### Word Choices

| Don't use | Use instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| log in / log out | sign in / sign out |
| click | select |
| tap | select |
| bank | card issuer |
| reserve (a slot) | book (a slot) |
| staff, workers | colleagues |
| profile | account |
| fees | charges (delivery charges, collection charges) |
| assist, assistance | help |
| help desk | service desk |
| modify | change |
| passcode, OTP, PIN code | code (or "6-digit code") |
| Marketplace partner | Marketplace seller (or just "seller") |
| fraud, suspicious activity | "we couldn't take your payment" (keep vague for security) |
| toggle | switch on / switch off, turn on / turn off |
| enable / disable | switch on / switch off, turn on / turn off |
| SMS | text |
| thank you | thanks |
| e.g. | for example, for instance |
| i.e. | in other words |
| etc. | and more |
| invalid | not working, not right, wrong |
| Ok, okay, O.K. | OK |
| just, only, simply (in directions) | Avoid — sounds dismissive or patronising |

### Formatting Rules

**Capitalisation:**
- Sentence case for everything: body copy, headings, buttons, links, error messages, notifications, form labels
- Title case only for navigational menus, breadcrumbs, and product names
- Capitalise specific section names ("Go to your Favourites") but lowercase in running copy ("Shop your favourites")

**Contractions:**
- Use contractions to stay conversational ("You'll get..." not "You will get...")
- Spell out if a contraction sounds awkward or could be misunderstood

**Numbers:**
- Use numerals by default (2, 4, 25) — exception: when two numbers run together and cause confusion
- Use commas for thousands (1,000 not 1000)
- Use % symbol (25% not "25 percent")

**Dates:**
- Long format: 1 January 2000, Saturday 1 January
- Short format: 1 Jan 2000, Sat 1 Jan
- Ranges: use hyphen with no spaces for same-month (1-2 January), spaces around hyphen for cross-month (1 January - 1 February)
- No ordinals (not 1st, 2nd, 3rd)
- No numeric-only dates (not 01/01/2000) — not accessible for screen readers. Exception: date pickers
- Don't include the year unless the date isn't this year

**Time:**
- 12-hour clock with am/pm, no space (1pm, 11am, 1.30pm)
- Full stop between hours and minutes, not colon (1.30pm not 1:30pm)
- No leading zeros (9am not 09am)
- No minutes when zero (1pm not 1.00pm)
- 12pm and 12am, not noon or midnight
- Ranges: am/pm for both times, hyphen with no spaces (7am-8am not 7-8am)
- Use "minutes" not "mins" unless space is very limited

**Colons and semicolons:**
- Avoid in UI copy — use shorter sentences and full stops instead

**Ampersands:**
- Use "and" in most cases. Use & only when space is very limited or in brand names (F&F)
- Never use "and/or" — rewrite for clarity

### Specific Terms

**check out / checkout:**
- "Checkout" = noun (the place/process: "before checkout")
- "Check out" = verb (the action: "check out our latest offers")

**choose / select:**
- "Choose" = customer decisions not UI-related
- "Select" = clickable/tappable UI decisions

**sign in / sign-in:**
- "Sign in" = verb (two words: "Sign in to continue")
- "Sign-in" = noun/adjective (hyphenated: "the sign-in screen")

**set up / setup:**
- "Set up" = verb ("Set up your account")
- "Setup" = noun/adjective ("Your account setup is complete")

**continue / next:**
- "Continue" = when consent is needed or choices will be made
- "Next" = shorter steps or going through content (onboarding)

**view / show:**
- "View" = navigates to a different page ("View all")
- "Show" = reveals content on the same page ("Show more")

**cost / price:**
- "Price" = what something costs before discounts
- "Cost" = what the customer actually pays

**product / item:**
- "Product" = a unique thing we stock
- "Item" = quantity of products (3 items of the same product)

**in-store:**
- Always hyphenated. Never "in store"

**upfront:**
- One word. Never "up-front" or "up front"

**please:**
- Avoid — implies there's a choice even when there isn't. State what the user needs to do directly.
- Exception: charity/donating copy

**sorry:**
- Only apologise when something's gone wrong on our end. Don't apologise for user errors.

**success / successfully:**
- Unnecessary. "Your password was reset" not "Your password was successfully reset"

**my / your (possessive pronouns):**
- Don't use in UI elements (page names, headings, breadcrumbs, menus, buttons, form labels)
- Ownership is already clear from context
- DO use in body copy when speaking to the user ("We've updated your account settings")
- Exception: feature names ("Your Clubcard Prices", "My List")

**OK (for buttons):**
- Use OK to acknowledge and close a message
- Don't use "Got it" (confusing for non-native speakers) or "Dismiss" (unclear outcome)

**Clubcard terminology:**
- Clubcard, Clubcard Prices, Clubcard points (specific capitalisation)
- Always "collect" points — never "earn" or "get"

**Click+Collect:**
- No spaces, no ampersand. Never "C+C" in customer-facing writing

## 12. What is Content Design

Content design is the practice of shaping information so people can find it, understand it, and act on it. It combines writing, information architecture, and user experience design.

### Why It Matters
- People don't read digital content word for word — they scan, skip, and focus on what stands out
- Every interaction competes with distractions, time pressure, and cognitive load
- Good content design reduces that load, helping people make decisions quickly and confidently

### The Science Behind It
- **Plain language** improves comprehension for everyone
- **Chunking** information into clear sections helps users scan and find what they need
- **Reading behaviour** — people read in short jumps (saccades) and pauses (fixations), scanning for key terms, headings, and visual anchors
- **Visual hierarchy** guides the user's eye to what's most relevant
- **F-pattern and layer-cake scanning** are common eye patterns — place key information accordingly
- **Cognitive load theory** — people process information better when presented simply
- **Information scent** — people decide whether to click or read based on clear, relevant cues

### How It Works in Practice
- Use testing, analytics, and best practice to understand how users interact with content
- Consider not just what the words are, but when, where, and how they're presented
- Content design overlaps heavily with UX and UI design — placement, labels, and sequence are all design decisions

## 13. Designing Content for Different Accessibility Needs

### Blind People
- Use screen readers, may use Braille output, won't use a pointer or mouse
- **Design for them:** meaningful alt text, clear headings, descriptive links, test with screen readers

### Low Vision / Visually Impaired People
- May use screen magnifiers, high-contrast modes, greyscale displays, or larger fonts
- Vision declines from age 40 — good contrast helps this large demographic
- **Design for them:** good colour contrast, readable fonts, consider content proximity when zoomed, test with magnifiers

### People with Physical Disabilities
- May use keyboards, speech recognition, track balls, or other assistive tech
- Many people prefer keyboard navigation for efficiency
- **Design for them:** design for keyboard interaction, descriptive links/page titles, visible button labels for speech recognition users

### People with Cognitive Disabilities
- May have challenges with memory, attention, reading, or comprehension
- Busy environments increase everyone's cognitive load
- **Design for them:** keep instructions visible throughout tasks, avoid flashing content, simple clear language, clean layouts

### Deaf People
- Rely on visual information; audio alerts may be missed
- English is often a second language for BSL users
- **Design for them:** caption all audio/video, visual alerts alongside sound, text-based communication options, plain language

### Deafblind People
- Mix of hearing and sight loss; may use screen reader + Braille display simultaneously
- **Design for them:** fully descriptive links and buttons (understood out of context), well-structured content with clear headers, don't embed text in images, provide transcripts, consider sign language

## 14. Conversation Design Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| ASR (Automated Speech Recognition) | System that transforms speech into written text understood by a language model |
| Barge-in | Feature allowing the user to interrupt the voice AI |
| Conversational turn / adjacency pair | The basic unit of conversation design — each speaker takes one turn |
| CUI (Conversation User Interface) | Interface that interacts with users via text or voice, imitating human conversations |
| CSAT (Customer Satisfaction Score) | Metric from post-interaction survey ("rate your experience 1-5") |
| IVR (Interactive Voice Response) | Automated phone system ("Press 1 for groceries, press 2 for Clubcard") |
| Fallback | Misunderstanding in AI communication; common message: "sorry I didn't catch that" |
| NPS (Net Promoter Score) | Metric measuring likelihood to recommend the service to others |
| Personality / Persona | The mental image users infer from the AI's voice and language choices (use "personality" not "persona" to avoid confusion with UX customer personas) |
| Repair | Methods to address and resolve communication problems; AI equivalent is "fallback" |
| TTS (Text to Speech) | System that transforms written text into synthetic voice |
| VUI (Voice User Interface) | An interface that interacts with users via voice |

## 15. Conversation Design Process (Step-by-Step)

1. **Specifications** — Product management provides general specs for a new conversation or feature
2. **Design document** — Speak with stakeholders; listen to and transcribe real human-human conversations of the same context
3. **Design the flow** — Create structure and goals; for LLM-based agents, work on the prompt (keep records of prompt iterations)
4. **Prototype** — Chat prototype in Figma; voice prototype using TTS tools + audio stitching
5. **Stakeholder review** — Share flow and prototype, revise based on feedback
6. **Formal approval** — Get sign-off from key stakeholders via email
7. **Development** — Work closely with developers on prompt design
8. **Testing** — UX testing ensures what's built reflects the design; raise defects; then UAT testing from business team
9. **Customer testing** — Enable for a percentage of customers; lead performance reviews (CSAT, success rate, handover rate, retention, conversational turns, fallback rate)
10. **Continuous improvement** — Iteration, iteration, and more iteration
